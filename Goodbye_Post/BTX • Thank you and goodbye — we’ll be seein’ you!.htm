Thank you and goodbye — we’ll be seein’ you!

Posted: Apr 20, 2023 4:07 pm
by btxadmin
A word of appreciation as we turn off the lights at these BTX forums.

Winding down the 43-year effort at Backstreets continues to be bittersweet, but as this stage of life nears an end, the great takeaway that keeps my spirits up — and that Backstreets has always been about, really — is the community I found here. The camaraderie, the lifelong friendships, the support I could always count on from fellow fans around the world, who I might never have met otherwise… those things don’t have to end. They’re the things I’ll take with me that mean the most.

I know BTX has engendered the same for so many people, and we’ll always be pleased and proud that fans found a community here. Despite whatever headaches these boards caused over the years (the spam, the trolls, the butting of heads — y’know, Internet message board stuff), and while other message boards shut down to escape those hassles (who remembers the “official” board at dot-net?), we always believed it was important to keep open this space for people to connect, to compare notes, to reflect, to help each other, to spread the news. Heck, for some people, to fall in love.

We found writers here. We found friends here. And generosity: one of my favorite memories is from 17 years ago, when a whole group of BTX-ers came together to surprise my wife and me with a special gift when our daughter was born — a custom-painted rocking horse, with our new baby's name and “Born in the U.S.A.” painted on the side. It’s one of the coolest gifts I’ve ever received. That baby is now 17, looking at colleges. Thank you to all of those who chipped in way back when and turned an idea into a keepsake that we’ll never let go of (unless and until, perhaps, we have a grandchild to ride it). For all the similar kindnesses and thoughtfulness here, thank you.

We did hear from people who are distraught or disappointed that these message boards are going away; I understand the feeling — endings are HARD. These past couple months I’ve been sorting through decades of Backstreets history here at HQ, surrounded by What I’ve Done With My Life from 22 to 52, and wrestling with feelings about this thing that has meant so much to me coming to an end. I can’t say I have regrets or second thoughts; all of us at Backstreets share the “end of an era” feeling and believe that closing is the right thing at the right time. Moreover, it’s meant enough to us that we don’t have any interest in handing the off keys in order to extend its life. But in the end, BTX is about the people, not a specific forum or URL: we’re confident that another gathering space will rise (or exists already) to fill the space.

Lastly, it’s worth reflecting on what sparked BTX — the Backstreets Ticket Exchange, lest anyone forgets — to begin with.

These message boards started in the late ’90s, but the idea for them came during the Ghost of Tom Joad tour. Backstreets HQ frequently received phone calls from fans either looking for tickets or looking to sell extras — enough that, rather than saying “sorry, nobody here needs those,” or “nobody has any extras here,” we started taking notes. We kept a yellow legal pad by the phone, and pages started to fill up with names and needs and phone numbers. Soon we had two pads going — one for “Ticket Needers,” one for “Ticket Holders.” And it was the coolest thing when we could connect somebody from one pad with somebody from the other.

So when we created Backstreets.com, setting up a message board to connect fans directly to facilitate those transactions on a larger scale was a high priority. In essence, we took those yellow legal pads and put them right online. Part of the idea was to keep tickets out of general circulation — really, out of the hands of scalpers (and later, out of the secondary marketplaces), to keep skyrocketing prices out of the equation, and to help as many fans as possible to see shows without breaking the bank. “Face value” was always the watchword. And no fees.

Back then, of course, Springsteen and his team made efforts to keep ticket prices low and to foil scalping, changing tactics some from tour to tour, maintaining others, and generally doing their best to help fans. Our Ticket Exchange was the fan-based solution: not perfect, but the best we could do to fight the same fight — the same battle on two fronts.

It may seem quaint that ours relied on the honor system… but it worked. With recent changes in the ticketing landscape, specifically dynamic pricing and the treatment of tickets as assets, a term like “face value” has gotten much hazier. Thus, it’s hard to know whether the premise of this exchange is sustainable in 2023 and beyond.

In any event, we want to thank everyone who used these boards in the way they were intended, for at least a couple of decades, and nine tours. Those who did prioritized helping their fellow fans over making a profit. It’s one of the legacies we’re proud of, BTX having paved the way for such efforts, with similar boards springing up ever since. It would never have worked without a fanbase with high ideals and convictions.

Special thanks to everyone who employed moderation here, from those users who chilled things out and cooled things down, to actual mods, especially main Moderator Flynn McLean, who endured plenty of slings and arrows while performing crucial functions in recent years that allowed BTX to continue when less patient heads might have pulled the plug. Tony Saddler, too, deserves thanks for his similar work and technical assistance in the early days of BTX.

Stay hard, stay hungry, stay alive, and we’ll see you in our dreams… or on some other forum just around the bend.

Chris
